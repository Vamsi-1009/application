Reliability & resilience — redundancy, backups, DDoS, and disaster recovery

Redundancy: the one idea behind everything below#

Every technique on this page is a variant of the same rule: never have exactly one of anything that can fail. One server, one database, one availability zone, one region, one person who knows how to restore a backup — each of these is a single point of failure, and a single point of failure eventually fails. Redundancy means there's always a second one ready to take over, and a way for the system (or a person) to notice and switch to it.

Multiple servers behind a load balancer#

Covered mechanically in Stage 10 — but the reliability framing matters as much as the scaling framing. The floor for a production system is two app instances, minimum, behind a load balancer that health-checks both. One instance is not "simple," it's a guaranteed outage the day that one VM has a bad disk, a kernel panic, or just needs a security patch reboot.

Database replication and automatic failover#

Stage 05 covered replication for read scaling (leader + read replicas). For reliability specifically, the detail that matters is how fast a replica becomes the new leader when the original dies. A self-managed replica needs a human to notice and manually promote it — minutes to hours, depending who's awake. A managed database with Multi-AZ enabled (RDS Multi-AZ, Cloud SQL high availability, etc.) does this automatically, typically within one to two minutes, at a real but usually worthwhile cost premium for anything customers depend on.

Backups: the discipline, not the checkbox#

Stage 05's checklist already says "an untested backup is a hope, not a plan" — here's what actually turning that into a plan looks like:

  • Automated, not manual. A human remembering to run a backup script is not a backup strategy.
  • Encrypted at rest, same as the live data.
  • Stored somewhere separate from the production account/region — if an attacker (or a mistake) can delete your live database, they should not also be able to delete its backups with the same credentials.
  • Retention matches a real requirement, not a guess — this is a different clock than Stage 16's CERT-In log-retention window; decide backup retention based on how far back the business actually needs to restore from, and document why.
  • Restore-tested on a schedule — a quarterly "restore this backup into a scratch environment and confirm the app boots against it" drill, with the time it took written down. That number is the closest real measurement you have of your actual recovery time, not the one on a slide.

Auto-restart & self-healing#

Stage 09's systemd unit (Restart=on-failure) and Stage 10's load-balancer health checks (an Auto Scaling Group terminates and replaces an instance that stops responding) are both instances of the same principle: a common failure should recover with zero human paged. Reserve people for the failures that are genuinely novel — a process crash or a hung instance shouldn't be one of them.

Rate limiting as a resilience control, not just an auth feature#

Stages 04 and 07 covered rate limiting to slow down brute-force and OTP abuse. The same mechanism, applied at the edge (load balancer or CDN/WAF) rather than just on auth routes, protects the whole app's capacity from being consumed — by an abusive user, a broken client stuck in a retry loop, or the opening minutes of a DDoS attempt before dedicated mitigation kicks in.

DDoS protection#

A Distributed Denial-of-Service attack throws far more traffic at you than any real user base would — either raw volume (flooding your network link), protocol-level tricks (exhausting connection tables), or targeted application-layer floods (hammering one expensive endpoint, like search or login, with traffic that looks legitimate). None of the techniques above are built to survive this on their own — autoscaling absorbs a legitimate traffic spike, but adding servers faster than an attacker can request them is a losing, expensive race.

Layer 1 — the CDN

Stage 08's CDN already absorbs a large share of volumetric traffic at the edge, far from your origin, simply by design.

Layer 2 — a managed DDoS service

AWS Shield Standard (free, automatic, always-on for every AWS customer) is the minimum baseline. Shield Advanced, or Cloudflare in front of the stack, adds active mitigation and support for business-critical traffic.

Layer 3 — WAF + rate limiting

Blocks application-layer floods that a network-level service can't distinguish from real traffic — rate-based WAF rules on your most expensive endpoints (search, login, checkout).

Layer 4 — autoscaling

Handles the legitimate spike that rides in alongside — a product launch, a viral post — once the actual attack traffic has been filtered out upstream.

Disaster recovery: planning for the day everything breaks#

Disaster recovery is Stage 13's incident response, extended to the worst case: not "one server is slow," but "the primary region is gone." Two numbers decide the whole plan, and they need deciding before an incident, not during one:

  • RTO (Recovery Time Objective) — how long can the business tolerate being down? An hour? A day?
  • RPO (Recovery Point Objective) — how much data can you afford to lose? Five minutes of writes? A full day's?

Multi-AZ protects against a single datacenter failing. Multi-region protects against an entire region or cloud provider having a bad day — a real but rare event, and meaningfully more expensive and complex to run continuously. Most teams don't need full multi-region active-active; they do need a written, versioned DR runbook — the exact commands to restore from backup into a fresh environment — and a "game day" where someone actually runs it end to end and times it, rather than only having it exist as an assumption.

Security checklist — reliability & resilience
  • Database failover has been tested at least once — not just configured NIST CSF · Recover
  • Backups are encrypted, stored separately from production, and restore-tested on a schedule NIST CSF · Recover
  • A managed DDoS protection service is enabled at the edge, at minimum the free automatic tier your cloud provider offers OWASP API Security · Unrestricted Resource Consumption
  • A written, versioned DR runbook exists with defined RTO/RPO and has been run end-to-end at least once NIST CSF · Recover
  • No single person is the only one who knows how to execute recovery NIST CSF · Respond
